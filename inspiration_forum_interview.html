Name Ed Griffin

Age 75

Fiona: Where are you from?
Ed: Born in Cleveland, Ohio and served as a Catholic Priest there for five years. Moved to Milwaukee, Wisconsin, where I met my wife and our children were born. Lived there for eighteen years. Moved to Surrey, British Columbia, Canada in 1988

Fiona: A little about your self `ie your education Family life ect 
Ed: Raised in a good Catholic family, I entered the seminary in first year high school. Twelve years later I was ordained. So I had four years of high school, four years of college, and four years of theology. During my priesthood, I took a one-year course in urban ministry from Western Reserve University. After I left the priesthood, I obtained a masters degree from the University of Wisconsin in community organization. So after university, I had seven more years of schooling. You’d think I knew something after all that.

Fiona: Tell us your latest news?
Ed: I’ve just signed up for KDP Select and I’m excited about that.

Fiona: When and why did you begin writing? 
Ed: In 1983, at the age of forty-seven, I discovered creative writing. It changed my life. I would sit down at the typewriter after supper and follow my creative muse. Whole worlds opened to me. I wrote about the space behind my childhood garage where I practiced pitching and dreamed of reaching the major leagues. I wrote a short story about a group of prisoners on an island. I wrote a poem about getting along with the Russians. Hours passed. Suddenly, as I wrote, an alarm would ring in the house. My wife and I owned a commercial greenhouse outside Milwaukee, Wisconsin, a ma-and-pa operation. The alarm meant I hadn’t turned the heat on. I had to shut the door on the vibrant world that grew on the paper in front of me and hurry to the greenhouses to start the furnaces. An hour later I’d be back at the typewriter. Type a sentence, stop, look at it, realize it wasn’t quite true and then search deeper, ever deeper. Layers of middle-aged half-truths disappeared, the comfortable maxims I had surrounded myself with – “Business is good. Don’t make any changes” and “Relax. You’re getting older.” The fires of my youth burned again – civil rights, world peace, a place in the sun for every person. I was a Catholic priest as a young man, filled with idealism. I marched in Selma with Doctor King, picketed local companies to give jobs to blacks, and confronted my own bishop over the church’s institutional racism.
As I wrote, I dug, I searched, always deeper, ever more honest. It might be easy to speak a lie, but it wasn’t easy to write one. I started to unravel the tangled skein that was me. What a wonderful gift this was. 

Fiona: When did you first consider yourself a writer? 
Ed: In 1985, the State of Wisconsin called for volunteers to teach creative writing in the prison system. I signed up and was assigned to the maximum-security prison at Waupun, Wisconsin. I’d never taught before, but as I looked at those twelve mean-looking dudes, I said, “My name is Ed Griffin. I’m a writer.”
In the next hour I learned how to teach. I knew there would be trouble, if I didn’t keep these guys interested and if I didn’t give them some good instruction. I’ve taught in prison ever since. 

Fiona: What inspired you to write your first book? 
Ed: From working with prisoners, I knew they were not a bunch of snarling, murdering convicts, as TV often portrays them. And I also knew that the prison system wasn’t helping them at all and was costing the taxpayer millions of dollars. Many staff were busy playing solitaire on their computers, as I noticed in the prison. Surely, there had to be a better way. I began thinking about putting these prisoners on an island with their families, with only the Coast Guard to keep them on the island. What would happen? Would they kill each other or would they set up a workable, if rough, society? And so I wrote my first book, a novel, Prisoners of the Williwaw. A williwaw is a fierce wind that happens in the Aleutian Islands.
Since then I have written four other books, two more novels and two books of non-fiction.
Beyond the Vows, a novel. What inspired me to write this book were the traumatic events of my young life, where I marched in Selma with Dr. Martin Luther King and was kicked out of a parish. The church that I had given my life to, did not stand up against racism. I turned this story into fiction.
Veto, a novel. I have long had an interest in the United Nations. I wrote a novel about a woman who becomes Secretary General of the UN, and turns this tired organization into an effective government to help people.
Dystopia, non-fiction. An inmate and I told our stories of prison. Mike Oulton was arrested in Mexico for smuggling drugs and spent two years in a Mexican prison and eight years in Canadian prisons. It’s interesting to see which one he liked better. And I wrote my story of teaching in prison. I wanted to teach men to write so they could tell the world of the dystopia they lived in.
Once A Priest is a themed autobiography, the theme being that I may have left the priesthood in 1968, but it hasn’t left me. When some people leave the church, they wipe it out of their lives. While I don’t talk about God or religion, I still carry with me the things I learned in the church – help the poor, work for peace and so forth.

Fiona: Do you have a specific writing style? 
Ed: Yes. Early on I determined that I wanted to write for ordinary people. The way I like to say it, is that I write for the supermarket, where popular, easy to read, page-turners are displayed right by the checkout counters. There’s lots of dialogue in my writing and lots of action. I’m often weak on description. I am not a literary writer.
Even though I know that eighty-five percent of what is written is non-fiction, the gods have made me a fiction writer and I just have to accept that.

Fiona: How did you come up with the title?
Ed: Prisoners of the Williwaw. While the men were no longer in prison, they were prisoners of the environment of the Aleutian Islands and they were often prisoners of their own hearts.

Fiona: Is there a message in your novel that you want readers to grasp? 
Ed: Yes. Convicts are not like what you see on TV. For four years I worked in a remand center, a pre-trial facility. A woman inmate read the book and said to me, “I know what you were trying to do.”
“Is that right? What did you think?’
“You were trying to say that convicts can do something worthwhile. And I really liked it that all your convicts were guilty. TV stories are often about an innocent man who gets caught in prison for one reason or another. All of your characters were guilty and yet they did something great.”
Better than ever before, this woman had it down perfect.

Fiona: How much of the book is realistic? 
Ed: There is a similar prison run by the Mexican Government called the Islas Marias off the coast of the Pacific. And my story is reminiscent of the history of Australia. 

Fiona: Are experiences based on someone you know, or events in your own life? 
Ed: No, they are based on the men and women I have known in twenty-three years of teaching in prison. 

Fiona: What books have most influenced your life most? 
Ed: One Flew Over the Cuckoos Nest by Ken Kesey
    On Writing Well, William Zinsser. 
    David Cooperfield by Charles Dickens
    The Writer’s Journey by Christopher Vogler
    The Elements of Style by Strunk and White
    Pacem in Terris by Pope John XXIII

Fiona: If you had to choose, which writer would you consider a mentor?
Ed: Hemmingway

Fiona: What book are you reading now? 
Ed: The Shelfless Book by Bob Mayer, Short Stories by Alice Munro

Fiona: Are there any new authors that have grasped your interest?
Ed: Christopher Vogler has taught me the hero’s journey and has introduced me to the work of Joseph Campbell and his study of mythology.

Fiona: What are your current projects? 
Ed: I’m working on two projects right now. One is a sequel to Prisoners of the Williwaw. The other is called Delaney’s Hope. People often say to me, “Griffin, you’re against prison. So what’s your answer?” I’m trying to write my answer, a novel in which Tom Delaney sets up an experimental prison with three staff and five inmates in a camp in northern Wisconsin. 

Fiona: Name one entity that you feel supported you outside of family members.
Ed: For over ten years, I’ve been a member of a dynamic writing group, called the Rainwriters. (It usually rains when we meet). We are only seven writers who meet every two weeks. We start on time and end on time – two hours. We never read at the meeting, rather we hand out work at the meeting to be reviewed at the next meeting.
This group has helped me with many writing problems and publishing problems. I know some people don’t work well in groups and I know some writing groups are just social gatherings but I couldn’t live without the Rainwriters.

Fiona: Do you see writing as a career? 
Ed: It’s been my career for the last twenty years, that and teaching writing. If I had to do my life over, I would have started writing much earlier. I’m glad that I have writing for my so-called retirement years.

Fiona: If you had to do it all over again, would you change anything in your latest book?
Ed: I don’t think so. I keep looking forward, not back.

Fiona: Do you recall how your interest in writing originated?
Ed: It’s always been at the back of my mind. Once – only once – in high school I wrote an essay that the teacher said was very good. But in my forties, as I relayed earlier, I was making good money in our greenhouse, but I knew my mind was atrophying. So I began to write.

Fiona: Is there anything you find particularly challenging in your writing? 
Ed: Description. If it was up to me, my novels would be dialogue and action. But I know I’m wrong and the women in the Rainwriters don’t hesitate to tell me I’m wrong. I have to help my readers feel that they are really on the scene. There’s a great quote – something like, “Don’t show me what the rain was like, make me feel wet.” I have to go back over my work and see if I’ve made the people and places come alive. Description, using all the senses, that’s a challenge for me.

Fiona: Who is your favorite author and what is it that really strikes you about their work? 
Ed: Dickens – I wish I could do characters like he does. Amazing. I can see David Cooperfield, Uriah Heep, Scrooge, etc. etc.

Fiona: Do you have to travel much concerning your book(s)? 
Ed: No, I don’t.

Fiona: Who designed the covers?
Ed: The art comes from a local artist, but all the work was done by my daughter and her husband.

Fiona: What was the hardest part of writing your book? 
Ed: The constant review. I write a novel the first time and review it with the Rainwriters. Then a second draft and review by the Rainwriters. Then a thorough review by my daughter, an English major, who corrects my grammar and tells me directly what things don’t work. Rewrite number three. Then I ask some special readers to look at the whole book and give me some feedback. This process takes a good three to four years.

Fiona: Did you learn anything from writing your book and what was it? 
Ed: I learned a lot from writing my last book, Once A Priest. When I looked at my own life, I discovered that I wasn’t the hero I always thought I was. I began to see things from other people’s perspectives.

Fiona: Do you have any advice for other writers? 
Ed: Writers write. They write when the feel inspired and when they don’t. Writers write.

Fiona: Do you have anything specific that you want to say to your readers?
Ed: Don’t ever let me get away with making a speech. Don’t let me preach. Stop me. Write to me. Kick me. 

Fiona: If you were not a writer what else would you like to have done?
Ed: Become a family doctor
Visit outer space
Become a crusading lawyer to help indigent people in prison
Become a great astronomer and teach astronomy in a university
Be a construction supervisor
Drive a big eighteen-wheeler truck
Become the Pope and radically change the Catholic religion

Ed: http://www.edgriffin.net

Quote:Delaney’s Hope

Chapter One
Opening Day

Shut it down. Shut it down. Tom Delaney could barely hear the reporter over the marchers’ protests. 
“Why a new kind of prison?” she yelled. “What’s wrong with what we have?” 
Suppressing his anger at the protest, he spoke into the black foam ball covering the microphone. “For one thing, prisons are costing the taxpayer a lot of money. It costs a hundred dollars a day to keep a man in prison.”
The newswoman glanced at her notes, while the cameraman panned the protestors blocking the gate to this rural experimental prison camp. Someone grabbed Delaney’s shoulder and pulled him backwards. He lost his balance and started to fall, but another shove pushed him forward. He ended up face down on the asphalt. A knee jammed into his back, pinning him down. 
“Fuckin’ do-gooder,” the person on his back shouted.
Someone kicked him hard on the leg.
Delaney’d seen a few cars arrive and then a mini-bus, loaded with people. His nemesis, Sheriff Barrick Matthews, directed the men and women where to march and handed each a sign. The men looked like they’d come from a labor pool, but the women appeared to be from a club. And, of course, the media were right behind. Why would a fellow correctional officer do this?
“Hey,” someone else shouted, “This place has a security wire around it. No wire, no prison. Rip it out.”
Another voice shouted, “Shovels in the trunk of my car.”
A cable carrying 440 volts lay next to the wire that could sense the transponder in an inmate’s shoulder. The 440 volts wire prohibited anyone from touching either wire. The voltage might not bother a person who used a wooden-handled shovel, but let there be a little water, or let the person grab the cable and – disaster.
Delaney struggled to get up. “The cable,” he yelled. The knee pushed him down harder.
“Let him up,” a voice said. It was Sheriff Matthews. “He’s a prison official.” 
Surprise. Matthews helping him. Then he heard Matthews mutter, “What the hell. It don’t matter.”
What was going to happen?
The newswoman did a frantic voice-over. “The protest against this new prison has turned violent. Warden Tom Delaney”
“Stop,” Delaney shouted at the men starting to dig for the wire. A gravel path with a red florescent line sprayed on it surrounded the prison, along with warning signs every hundred feet.
Delaney hobbled over to the men, the pain where he’d been kicked shooting through his leg. The knee out of his new slacks. “You have to stop. You could be electrocuted.”
“We’re gonna shut you down,” a man said. “This place is gonna wreck our town.” He kept digging.
The camp was ten miles from Maria River. A small town in northern Wisconsin, it had lost jobs, but Tom didn’t see how his experimental prison was going to wreck the town. 
A vehicle approached on the rural Wisconsin highway. It was a sheriff’s van carrying his first prisoners. When it pulled up to the protest line, Barrick Matthews strode over to it and talked to the driver. Delaney limped over to the van as well, the newswoman right behind him. This day couldn’t be going worse, he thought.
“You can’t enter,” Sheriff Matthews said.
The driver responded, “I’ve got five prisoners here. Four men and one woman.”
Matthews spat on the ground. “Men and women together. Holy shit.”
“Let that van through,” Delaney called out. “This is a court-ordered transfer.”
“I’m in charge,” Matthews shouted. “Turn around. This camp don’t meet State standards.”
Delaney pushed himself forward to the van window. “Go on in, Driver. I’ve had this camp inspected.” He turned eyeball to eyeball to the Sheriff. “You’re wrong, Sheriff. Step away.”
Matthews pushed Delaney aside. “I’m the Sheriff here. My opinion counts. The place isn’t fit. Turn around.”
Delaney’s heart sank as he watched the van turn around and head back toward Maria River. The protestors cheered. The first day of his new prison and – disaster. His dream – a prison that really worked – defeated before it even opened. Never mind digging up the cable. He was already shut down.
He limped back down the entrance road to the warden’s cottage. He could still feel the imprint of the protestor’s knee in his back. But that wasn’t the worst thing – he had failed: failed to win Sheriff Matthews over, failed to build a group of supporters in the town, and failed to win over the local media. He was too impatient to get started, unwilling to slow the train down so others could hop aboard. Crystal, his ex, used to say, “Tom, you see a problem and you jump. You don’t think it through.” 
His dream – gone. He wanted a prison that would melt the bars surrounding an inmate’s soul. For five years he’d planned this prison and done little else. He’d visited prisons with exciting programs in Europe, studied dense criminology papers and talked to experts. He’d written reports and bounced them back to the experts for further comment. As well, he’d sent the plan to inmates he knew for their suggestions. Redraft followed redraft and now the plan was ready and about to be implemented, when – failure.
He trudged forward. At least the protestors had stopped trying to dig up the wire. 
But wait. He stopped walking. Maybe he could make it happen. One of his key staff, Al Casey, was still in Maria River. 
He punched a number on his cell phone. 


Delaney retreated to the warden’s cottage to recover. He was a six-footer, still handsome in his early fifties, with a generous head of black hair, gray scattered throughout. He sat at his small kitchen table with a cup of coffee. The cottage was one level, small everything, but adequate for his needs. 
Tom picked up the Maria River Times. The paper quoted the county commissioner, “Maria River needs this federal experiment like it needs a dozen tornadoes. Let Delaney coddle his criminals somewhere else.”
Coddle criminals. That was the exact opposite of what he was doing. He was sticking it to them for the first time in two hundred years of American criminology. His plan was simple: No more do your time and get out. Now it was you get out when you change. 
Coddle criminals! His face reddened and he stood up to relieve the anger. 
He took his coffee and walked to the window. Wisconsin spring air rolled in from the glaciated hills, mixed with a hint of a farmer’s pungent fertilizer. Spring always brought him hope and today his dream might still happen. He took a big breath to further calm himself.
A frown crossed his brow. Could he do it? He wasn’t getting any younger. Wheels were always coming off experiments and this one was a revolution. The director of prisons in Washington, Mr. Kierney, had made his position clear. “I’m against this project, Mr. Delaney, but Congress wants experiments with new forms of prisons. One year only.”
Delaney knew that Kierney wanted to give the contract to Sheriff Matthews, who had submitted a proposal as he did. Matthews’ proposal was for a maximum-security prison in Maria River, holding thousands of prisoners and employing thousands of staff. But Congress overruled Director Kierney.
Looking up the road, Delaney saw that the media truck and the protestors had left.
He made another call on his cell phone and put on his jacket. It was a chilly first of April in northern Wisconsin. Hell of a day for a new prison to open, he thought. April Fool’s Day. Certain officials were, no doubt, enjoying a good joke on him. One of his colleagues had said, “Delaney wants to bring democracy to Neanderthals. He’ll learn.” 
His back hurt. As he reached for the doorknob, the house phone rang. He answered it. “Tom…I mean, Maria River Prison Camp.”
“Hello, is this Mr. Delaney?”
“Yes?”
“This is Anne-Marie Oakhurst. I’m the mayor of Maria River. I want to know if you’re all right?”
The woman’s voice surprised him. It flowed gently out of the receiver like a stream, but there was firmness in the voice as well. 
“Ah, what do you mean, Mayor?”
“The demonstration this morning. I saw it on TV. I saw you being shoved to the ground and kicked. I want you to know, Mr. Delaney, that I oppose your prison experiment, but I don’t approve of those tactics. I hope you’re all right.”
Words of calmness, rationality. That’s how Crystal used to talk in the early days, when he struggled to bring decent educational programs to the men.
“Thank you, Mayor. I’m fine.”
“Sheriff Matthews – his heart is in the right place, but he gets carried away. I’ve no idea where he got those protestors.”
Delaney said nothing. Matthews’ heart was in big, cement and steel prisons, men in cages, one staff for every inmate. Constant lockdown and no rehabilitation.
The mayor continued. “Our area needs jobs, Mr. Delaney. We lost a shoe factory to China and an auto parts factory to Mexico. That’s why Sheriff Matthews and I wanted a big prison here.”
“I understand…what do I call you? Madam Mayor? Your Honor?”
She laughed. “Anne-Marie…, just Anne-Marie. And you?”
“Tom. Tom Delaney.” 
“Well, I’m glad to hear you’re all right, Tom.”
“You know, Mayor, … this camp will bring a lot of good jobs to Maria River, counselors, psychologists, parole officers and teachers. We just need time.”
“Yes, well we’ll just have to see. Nice talking to you, Tom.”
She hung up.
Delaney put the phone down. The woman seemed confident in her own authority. She didn’t need people to bow and scrape and call her ‘Your Honor.’ She knew who she was – Anne-Marie.
As he walked out to the road, he realized that he should have sat down with the mayor beforehand – the mayor and a lot of other people. He kicked himself again for not preparing the community for an innovative prison.
A patch of crocuses had sprung up near the cottage. Sign of hope, he thought. He placed another call on his cell phone and walked toward the entrance, thoughts flooding his mind: the Department of Corrections awarding him a plaque for his twenty years of educational leadership. That was a joke, and the gold plaque on his kitchen wall reminded him at every lonely meal what a phony he was. Except for his first two years, he’d just coasted, showed up for work and collected his paycheck. EXCELLENCE the plaque said. His missionary brother – that was a life of excellence. The picture came back to him as it always did – his brother on a slab in a morgue in Columbia, shot by the private troops of a rich landowner. 
Yes, his brother on a slab. A martyr. That was the day he changed. No more easy life, golfing, sailing, collecting the prison paycheck. Five years of planning, now he had to succeed.
The camp entrance was clear. He called again on his cell phone and a few minutes later the same sheriff’s van drove up and stopped at the entrance. He walked over to the driver’s side. “Good work, Al. They all here?”
“Yep,” Al said. “Four men and one woman.”
A policeman sat in the passenger seat. “I appreciate you coming along on this trip,” Delaney said.
The officer replied, “’Bout time someone tried something new. Good luck to you, Sir.”
“Drive on, Al. I’ll meet you in the big A-frame building in half an hour. The woman will stay in the cottage with Kali.”
Al laughed. “Got to get all the chains off them first.”
The van drove into the camp and Tom walked back down the road. He had his experimental camp and his first five prisoners.
Time to make it happen.
